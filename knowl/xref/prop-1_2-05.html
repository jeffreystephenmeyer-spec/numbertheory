Proposition 1.3.6. .

If \(R\) is a ordered ring whose positive elements are well-ordered, then the minimal positive element, \(e\) is (multiplicatively) invertible.
🔗
🔗

Hint.

Try a direct proof. Let \(e\) denote the minimal positive element of \(R\text{.}\) (Why does \(e\) exist???) If \(e=1\text{,}\) we are done (why???). Otherwise \(0< e < 1\text{.}\) Look at the set
\begin{equation*} S = \{1-ke >0 \mid k \in \Z \} \end{equation*}
\(S\) is nonempty (why???). There exists a minimal element \(1-ne\in S\text{.}\) With minimality in mind, can \(1-ne> e\text{?}\) Can \(1-ne< e\text{?}\) Show \(1-ne=e\) (why???). Use this to find an element \(m\in \Z\) such that \(me=1\) (how???). You may find Definition 1.2.1, Definition 1.3.2, Definition 1.3.5 useful.
