    Section 2.2 GCD

    🔗

    Definition 2.2.1. Common Divisor.

    If \(a \) and \(b \) are integers and there is an integer \(d \) that divides both of them, then \(d \) is a common divisor of \(a \) and \(b \text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.2. Greatest Common Divisor (GCD).

    If \(a \) and \(b \) are integers, if \(g \) is a common divisor of \(a \) and \(b \) such that if \(d \) is any other common divisor of \(a \) and \(b \text{,}\) then \(g \ge d\text{,}\) then \(g \) is the greatest common divisor (gcd) of \(a \) and \(b \text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We denote the gcd of integers \(a \) and \(b \) by \(\gcd(a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The following proposition is foundational for the rest of this chapter. It is a consequence of the division algorithm and the well-ordering principle.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.2.3. GCD and the Division Algorithm.

    If \(a,b,q,r\in \Z\) where \(b > 0\) and \(a = bq+r \text{,}\) then \(\gcd(a,b) = \gcd(b,r)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a direct proof. First show that any common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\) is also a common divisor of \(b\) and \(r\text{.}\) Then show the converse. You may find Definition 2.1.1, Definition 2.2.1, Definition 2.2.2, Proposition 2.1.5 useful.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 2.2.4. The Euclidean Algorithm.

    If \(a,b\in \Z\) where \(b > 0\text{,}\) then using the division algorithm repeatedly:
    \begin{align*} a \amp= bq_1 + r_1 \amp\amp\text{where } 0 \le r_1 < b,\\ b \amp= r_1 q_2 + r_2 \amp\amp\text{where }0 \le r_2 < r_1,\\ r_1 \amp= r_2 q_3 + r_3 \amp\amp\text{where }0 \le r_3 < r_2,\\ \amp\vdots \amp\amp\\ r_{n-2} \amp= r_{n-1} q_{n} + r_n \amp\amp\text{where } 0 \le r_n < r_{n-1},\\ r_{n-1} \amp= r_n q_{n+1}. \amp\amp \end{align*}
    Let \(r_{n}\) be the last positive remainder. Then \(\gcd(a,b) = r_n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a direct proof. How do you know the process terminates? Can you find a nonempty decreasing sequences of positive integers? You may find Proposition 2.1.6, Definition 1.3.1, and Proposition 2.2.3 useful.
    🔗
    🔗
    The Euclidean Algorithm is truly an amazing algorithm. If you go and inspect your proof, you will see that you iterate the division algorithm no more than \(2\log_2(b)+1\) times! This is a very efficient algorithm for computing the gcd of two integers. Even though it is over two thousand years old, it is still state-of-the-art and has profound applications in number theory and beyond.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.2.5. Bezout Identity.

    If \(a,b\in \Z\) then there exists \(x,y\in \Z\) so that \(ax+by = \gcd(a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a constructive direct proof. Use the Euclidean Algorithm to find the gcd and then work backwards to find the coefficients. You may find Proposition 2.2.4 useful.
    🔗
    🔗
    This proof strategy is called the extended Euclidean algorithm. It is an efficient way to find the gcd of two integers and also find the coefficients in Bezout’s identity. We will use this algorithm throughout this book.
    🔗
    While this may all seem very theoretical, it is also very concrete and gives explicit steps for computing the gcd and finding the coefficients in Bezout’s identity. These algorithms are used in many applications, including cryptography, coding theory, and computational number theory.
    🔗

    Activity 2.2.1. Computing the GCD and Bezout Coefficients.

    Use the extended Euclidean algorithm to find integers \(x\) and \(y\) such that \(13x + 8y = \gcd(13,8)\text{.}\)
