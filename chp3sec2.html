    Section 3.2 Prime Factorization

    🔗

    Proposition 3.2.1. Prime Factorization (Existence).

    Every integer greater than 1 can be expressed as a product of prime numbers.
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Let \(X\) be the set of all positive integers greater than 1 that cannot be expressed as a product of prime numbers. Try a proof by contradiction and assume \(X\) is nonempty. Then \(X\) has a minimal element, say \(n\) (why???). \(n\) cannot be prime (why???), so it is a product of an integer greater than 1 and a prime (why???). Leverage minimality to deduce a contradiction. You may find Definition 1.3.1, Definition 3.1.1, Definition 2.1.1, Proposition 3.1.5 and Proposition 3.1.3 useful.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 3.2.2. Euclid’s Lemma.

    If \(p\in \Z\) is prime and \(p \mid bc\) for some \(b,c \in \Z\text{,}\) then \(p \mid b\) or \(p \mid c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a direct proof. Suppose \(p \nmid b\text{.}\) The \(\gcd(p,b)=1\) (why???). Deduce that there exist integers \(x,y\in \Z\) such that \(px+by=1\text{.}\) Use this to show that \(p \mid c\text{.}\) You may find Definition 3.1.1, Definition 2.2.2, Definition 2.1.1, Proposition 2.2.5 and Proposition 2.1.5 useful.
    🔗
    🔗
    The property stated in Euclid’s Lemma is not just a consequence of the definition of prime numbers; it is a deeper property that distinguishes prime numbers from composite numbers. In fact, the converse of Euclid’s Lemma is also true.
    🔗

    Proposition 3.2.3. Euclid’s Converse.

    If \(a\in \Z\) is a number greater than 1 with the property that whenever \(a \mid bc\) for some \(b,c \in \Z\text{,}\) then \(a \mid b\) or \(a \mid c\text{,}\) then \(a\) is prime.
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a direct proof. Suppose \(d \mid a\text{.}\) Then there exists a \(q\in \Z\) such that \(a=dq\text{.}\) Either \(a \mid d\) or \(a \mid q\text{.}\) Analyze both cases and deduce that \(d\) must either be 1 or \(a\text{.}\) You may find Definition 2.1.1, Proposition 3.1.4, and Definition 3.1.1 useful.
    🔗
    🔗
    Euclid’s Lemma (Proposition 3.2.2)is a fundamental property of prime numbers that plays a crucial role in number theory, particularly in the proof of the uniqueness of prime factorization.
    🔗

    Proposition 3.2.4. Prime Factorization (Uniqueness).

    The prime factorization of a positive integer greater than 1 is unique up to the order of the factors.
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Suppose that a positive integer \(n\) has two prime factorizations:
    \begin{align*} n \amp = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_k\\ n \amp = q_1 q_2 \cdots q_m \end{align*}
    where \(p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_k\) and \(q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_m\) are prime numbers. You want to show that \(k=m\) and that the sets of primes are the same (up to order). Show that \(p_1\) must divide one of the \(q_i\)’s, up to relabelling \(q_1\text{.}\) Deduce that \(p_1 = q_1\text{.}\) We can then cancel \(p_1\) and \(q_1\) from both factorizations and repeat the argument to show that \(p_2\) must, up to relabelling, equal \(q_2\text{,}\) and so on. You may find Definition 2.1.1, Definition 3.1.1, Proposition 3.2.2, Proposition 1.3.7 useful.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 3.2.5. Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic.

    Every positive integer greater than 1 can be uniquely expressed as a product of prime numbers, up to the order of the factors.
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    You may find Proposition 3.2.1 and Proposition 3.2.4 useful.
