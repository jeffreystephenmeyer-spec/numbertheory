    Section 2.4 Congruences

    🔗

    Definition 2.4.1. Congruence.

    If \(a \) and \(b \) are integers and \(m \) is a positive integer, then \(a \) and \(b \) are congruent modulo \(m \text{,}\) written \(a \equiv b \pmod m\text{,}\) if \(m \mid (a-b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 2.4.2. Congruence is an Equivalence Relation.

    If \(a,b,c,m\in \Z\) where \(m>0\text{,}\) then
    1. \(\displaystyle a \equiv a \pmod m\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(a \equiv b \pmod m\text{,}\) then \(b \equiv a \pmod m\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. If \(a \equiv b \pmod m\) and \(b \equiv c \pmod m\text{,}\) then \(a \equiv c \pmod m\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a direct proof. Let the definitions structure your proof. You may find Definition 2.4.1, Definition 2.1.1, Proposition 2.1.2, and Proposition 2.1.3 useful.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 2.4.3. Congruence Preserves Operations.

    If \(a,b,a',b'\in \Z\) where \(m>0\) and \(a \equiv a' \pmod m\) and \(b \equiv b' \pmod m\text{,}\) then
    1. \(\displaystyle a+b \equiv a' + b' \pmod m\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle ab \equiv a'b' \pmod m\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a direct proof. Let the definitions structure your proof. You may find Definition 2.4.1, Definition 2.1.1, and Proposition 2.4.2 useful.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.4.4. Least Residue.

    If \(a \) and \(m \) are integers and \(m \) is a positive integer, the unique \(r \) from the division algorithm such that \(a=qm+r\) and \(0\le r < m\) is called the least residue of \(a\) modulo \(m \text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 2.4.5. Congruence and Least Residue.

    If \(m\in \Z\) is positive, then two integers \(a\) and \(b\) are congruent modulo \(m\) if and only if they have the same least residue modulo \(m\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a direct proof. First suppose two integers \(a\) and \(b\) have the same least residue modulo \(m\text{.}\) Write down the expressions and analyze. Why are they congruent modulo \(m\text{?}\) Next suppose the integers are congruent modulo \(m\) and let \(r\) be the least residue of one of them. Why must the other have the same least residue modulo \(m\text{?}\) You may find Definition 2.4.1, Definition 2.4.4, Definition 2.1.1, and Proposition 2.4.3 useful.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 2.4.6. Cancellation Theorem for Congruences.

    If \(a,m\in \Z\) are positive relatively prime integers and \(x,y,\in \Z\text{,}\) then \(ax \equiv ay \pmod m\) implies \(x \equiv y \pmod m\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a direct proof. Try rewriting the congruence. What is \(ax - ay\) congruent to? Why? You may find Definition 2.4.1, Definition 1.1.1, Definition 2.1.1, Definition 2.3.4, and Proposition 2.4.3 useful.
