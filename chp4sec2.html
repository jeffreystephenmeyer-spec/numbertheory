    Section 4.3 Wilson’s Theorem

    🔗

    Definition 4.3.1. Factorial.

    The factorial of n, denoted \(n!\text{,}\) is the product of all positive integers less than or equal to \(n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 4.3.2. Squares of Roots of One Modulo a Prime.

    If \(p\) is a prime and \(r\) is an integer, then \(r^2 \equiv 1 \pmod p\) if and only if \(r \equiv 1 \pmod p\) or \(r \equiv -1 \pmod p\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a direct proof. Begin with the forward direction. Rewrite this as \(r^2 - 1 \equiv 0 \pmod p\text{.}\) Factor the left and analyze. The other direction is immediate. You may find Proposition 2.4.3, Definition 3.1.1, Definition 2.4.1, and Proposition 3.2.2 useful.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 4.3.3. Wilson’s Theorem.

    If \(p\) is a prime, then \((p-1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a direct proof. Check \(p=2,3\) by hand. Now suppose \(p \ge 5\text{.}\) The big idea is you will want to group the elements of the product \(1 \cdot 2 \cdot \cdots \cdot (p-1)\) in a useful way. You may find it useful to observe that \((p-1)!=(p-1)\cdot (p-2)!\) and compute \((p-2)!\) first. Every least residue modulo \(p\) has a unique multiplicative inverse modulo \(p\) (why???). When are these distinct? When they are distinct, pair an element with its inverse. You may find Definition 1.1.1, Proposition 3.4.2, Proposition 4.3.2, Definition 3.4.1, and Definition 2.4.1 useful.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 4.3.4. Wilson’s Converse Theorem.

    If \(n\) is a not prime, then \((n-1)! \not\equiv -1 \pmod{n}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Hint.

    Try a direct proof by cases. First check \(n=4\text{.}\) Next suppose \(n=p^2\) where \(p\) is a prime greater than 2. Then \(p\) and \(2p\) are both distinct least residues modulo \(n\) (why???). What are the implications? Lastly, suppose \(n\) is not the square of a prime. Then there are distinct least residues \(a\) and \(b\) modulo \(n\) such that \(ab = n\) (why???). What are the implications? You may find Definition 3.1.1 and Definition 2.4.1 useful.
